# Common Senior Behavioral Questions

## Leadership & Ownership

### "Describe a time you led a technically complex project."
**What they want to hear:**
- How you broke down ambiguity into tasks
- Technical decisions you owned
- How you communicated progress and risks to stakeholders
- What you would do differently

**Preparation tip**: Prepare a project story with: problem scale, your specific architecture/design decisions, team size, timeline, and business outcome.

---

### "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager or team and what happened."
**What they want to hear:**
- You can advocate for your technical position respectfully
- You use data/reasoning, not just opinion
- You can commit to a decision even if you disagree (disagree and commit)
- You don't hold grudges

**Example angle**: "We were about to adopt a microservice for a component I thought should remain a module. I prepared a written comparison of the operational overhead vs the coupling benefit. We had a structured discussion, and ultimately the team chose to keep it as a module with a cleaner interface. Six months later, this turned out to be the right decision as we didn't need independent scaling."

---

## Technical Decision-Making

### "Tell me about a time you had to make an architectural decision under time pressure."
**What they want to hear:**
- You made a conscious trade-off (perfect vs good enough)
- You documented the trade-off as tech debt with a plan
- You monitored the outcome

---

### "Describe a situation where you had to refactor a large legacy codebase."
**What they want to hear:**
- Strategy: strangler fig, seam stitching, or big bang?
- How you maintained stability during refactoring (feature flags, canary releases)
- Metrics for success (test coverage, deployment frequency, bug rate)

---

## Handling Failure

### "Tell me about your biggest professional failure."
**What they want to hear:**
- Honesty (not "my failure was working too hard")
- Clear ownership: "I made a mistake by…"
- What you learned
- What systems/processes changed as a result

**Example**: "I pushed a configuration change on a Friday afternoon without going through our deployment checklist because I thought it was minor. It caused a 45-minute outage. I learned to treat all production changes with the same process rigor regardless of perceived size. I then led the effort to automate our deployment checklist into our CI pipeline."

---

## Collaboration & Communication

### "Describe a time you mentored a junior developer."
**What they want to hear:**
- Structured approach to knowledge transfer
- Patience and adaptation to the learner's level
- Business outcome (team throughput, quality improvement)

---

### "Tell me about a time you worked with a difficult stakeholder."
**What they want to hear:**
- Empathy: what were their real concerns?
- How you communicated technical constraints in business terms
- Outcome: a solution both could commit to

---

## Questions to Ask the Interviewer

At the end of senior interviews, always ask 2-3 thoughtful questions:

1. *"What does the on-call rotation look like for this team, and what was the last major incident?"* — Shows production-mindset.
2. *"How does the team make architectural decisions? Is it consensus-driven or does a tech lead decide?"* — Shows interest in process.
3. *"What are the biggest technical challenges the team is facing right now?"* — Shows genuine curiosity and lets you assess fit.
4. *"What does the definition of 'done' look like for a new feature?"* — Reveals engineering culture.
5. *"How do you measure the impact of engineering work?"* — Reveals data-driven culture.

---

## Behavioral Interview Checklist

Before the interview, prepare stories covering:
- [ ] A technical decision you're proud of
- [ ] A production incident you resolved
- [ ] A time you improved a process
- [ ] A failed project and what you learned
- [ ] A time you mentored someone
- [ ] A disagreement and how you resolved it
- [ ] A time you worked under tight deadlines
- [ ] Your biggest technical mistake
